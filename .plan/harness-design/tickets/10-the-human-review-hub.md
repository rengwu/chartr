# The human review hub

## Question

The moment the whole design turns on. A ticket is `proposed` and the human must decide. This is not a modal yes/no — it is a hub with four exits: **approve**, **take it further** (more grilling, ad-hoc improvements, asking the model for advice), **abandon/discard/re-grill**, and arriving here *forced*, because an agent-review rejection always halts to a human and never loops.

Make it concrete, and settle:

- **What the human is shown, and in what order:** the diff, the `## Proposed Answer`, the ticket's Done-when, the agent-review verdict. What does a useful verdict summary look like — and how does the human tell "the reviewer is nitpicking" from "the reviewer found a real bug"? A gate people rubber-stamp is not a gate, and this is the surface that decides which one it becomes.
- **How taking it further works.** It spawns more sessions against the same ticket, each adding commits, until the human is satisfied. How does the human see what has accumulated since the original proposal, and does the `## Proposed Answer` get rewritten as it goes?
- **How approve feels.** Approving is what promotes `## Proposed Answer` to `## Answer` and unblocks the ticket's dependents — the single act that lets work compound, and the only containment against a wrong ticket seeding its successors. It should not be a button anyone clicks by accident, and it should not be so heavy that people stop reading.
- **What abandon asks for and what it destroys.** Ticket 06 owns the git mechanics; this owns the human's side of it.
- Where the **"start the next best frontier ticket"** offer appears once approval lands, and whether it is a suggestion or a shove.

## Answer

**The hub is a brief, not a dossier: one screen that answers three questions — what was done, what the reviewer found, what to do next — with everything else one click deeper.** Prototyped as four variants ([the asset](../assets/10-human-review-hub.html); **D "Brief" is canonical**, A/B/C kept as round one's record). It takes over the map card, entered by 08's routes: the reviews-first action station or a proposed star's sheet. *What was done* is the `## Proposed Answer` verbatim — the claim the agent already wrote is the summary, no new machinery. *What the reviewer found* is one line ("Rejected — 1 blocking, 1 advisory") plus the blocking finding's sentence; the per-clause Done-when check and full findings sit behind a "Full verdict" expander, the diff behind a collapsed "N files, +M lines" row that the blocking finding's filename jumps into. Round one's contract-first dossier (A), disposition-gated approve (B) and PR-style diff-first read (C) were rejected at review as intimidating: the gate's protection moved from *ceremony* into *anchoring*.

**The verdict is anchored to the contract — which is how nitpicking reads differently from a real bug.** The reviewer must assess every Done-when clause (met/unmet), and a finding may only *block* by citing the clause it breaks; an unanchored finding is advisory **by rule**. The recommendation the hub leads with is **derived mechanically from the verdict by the harness** — reject → "send back to fix", pass → "ready to approve", consequences named ("approving unblocks #05") — never free text from a model: no agent editorializes at the human's gate.

**Approve is one click, plus exactly one tick when the reviewer said no.** The button names its outcome ("Approve — resolve #06") and a quiet consequence line states the mechanics: promotes `## Proposed Answer` → `## Answer` as its own pathspec-limited commit (ticket 06), and what it unblocks. A rejecting verdict adds a single acknowledgement — "I've read the blocking finding" — before approve enables, and approving over a rejection is recorded in the promotion commit. B's per-finding waiver prose was rejected as the right instinct at the wrong weight.

**Taking it further accumulates on the proposal, and send-back is the human's feedback channel.** Follow-up sessions (fix-up, grill, or commit-less advice) run against the same still-proposed ticket; commits stack, the `## Proposed Answer` is rewritten in place (priors live in git), and the diff offers three scopes: all commits / since the verdict / since your last read. "Send back to fix" opens a briefing dialog showing exactly what the fix-up session will be told — the standard bundle, the blocking finding always, advisories opt-in — plus an **optional human note that travels in the injected payload and its archive (ticket 04), never the ticket file**. The asymmetry is deliberate: a send-back note steers a live continuation that has full context, while abandon's reason must survive for a fresh future attempt — so only abandonment writes into the ticket.

**Abandon asks for one thing and destroys nothing.** Its dialog requires a rejection reason *addressed to the next attempt* (it becomes the `### Rejected` prose the next session's bundle carries — ticket 06), states plainly what it will and won't do, and offers reverting the work commits as an unticked lever. An agent-review rejection is a forced stop announced by a one-line banner: it halts to the human and never loops.

**The next-best-ticket offer is a suggestion, never a shove.** After approval the hub collapses into a strip — what was promoted, what it unblocked, and the top frontier ticket (freshly unblocked dependents first, then by unblock count) — whose spawn button enables only 450ms after approve, so it can never inherit the click; Esc dismisses it. Walking away from the hub itself is equally legal; the action-station badge keeps count.

**The brief is plain markdown on disk; the GUI adds buttons and nothing else.** The harness assembles it like ticket 04's payloads to a known path in the space, so a TUI user reads the identical text (`{ } raw` in the prototype shows the file). That keeps the hub inside the hackability preference and leaves a pure-CLI review flow open without a second rendering path. The prototype is throwaway; the decisions above are the deliverable.
